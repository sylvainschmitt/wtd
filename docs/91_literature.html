Literature

Flavia R. C. Costa et al. (2022)

Abstract:

  • Tropical forest function determined by water availability
  • Groundwater related to soil water through the water table depth (WT)
  • Shallow WT forests (WT < 5 m) underrepresented in research
  • Shallow WT ~50% Amazon & different response CC droughts
  • Review WT patterns and consequences + conceptual model (env, traits, CC)
  • Shallow WT distinct composition: resource-acquisitive, hydrologically vulnerable, shorter canopies and lower biomass.
  • ‘normal’ years shallow WT forests have higher mortality and lower productivity
  • moderate droughts mortality is buffered and productivity increases
  • severe drought more sensitive due to shallow roots and drought-intolerant traits?

Notes:

  • lack of WT variations in Dynamic Vegetation Models
  • Forest dyn | CC ~ f( WT x Clim x FT)
  • To sim Delta WT TROLL needs stomatal control improvement that we plan to dev for rainfall exclusion experiment
  • Shallow WT results in shallower roots due to hypoxia or anoxia (to dev)
  • Low runoff in the Amazon with ref
  • Wt driven by topo x clim
  • Capillary water uprise can go from <1m in sand to few metres in clay
  • Anoxic conditions > from root respiration to alcoholic fermentation > 1 vs 36 ATP prod per glycose > limit root growth > reduce root permeability > reduce stomatal conductance > reduce photosysnthesis
  • Anoxic conditions > reduce available nitrogen
  • Shallower roots > higher uprooting
  • Agenda: (1) understanding shallow WT productivity shifts, (2) explore a potential tipping point, (3) link WTD, WUE, CO2 enrichment and phosphorous availability, (4) predict forest responses to CC with field and remote sensing data

Aleixo et al. (2019)

Abstract:

  • Increasing mortality in tropical moist forests
  • Importance of climate and speceis traits evaluated with survival >1,000 trees, >200 species, >50year in the Central Amazon
  • Drought, heat, storms and extreme rainy years increase mortality to at least two years
  • Pioneers, softwoods and evergreens are especially sensitive
  • This may result in faster carbon sequestration but lower carbon storage

Cosme et al. (2017)

Abstract:

  • local filtering of species link to hydraulic architectural traits?
  • hydrological microhabitat partitioning?
  • hydraulic characteristics at tissue (anatomical traits, wood specific gravity (WSG)), organ (leaf area, specific leaf area (SLA), leaf area : sapwood area ratio) and whole-plant (height) levels for 28 pairs of congeneric species specialized in valleys or plateaus
  • On plateaus, higher WSG, lower mean vessel area, mean vessel hydraulic diameter, sapwood area and SLA; traits commonly associated with hydraulic safety
  • 2 strategies for either efficiency or safety were detected, based on vessel size or allocation to sapwood

Flávia Regina Capellotto Costa et al. (2020)

Abstract:

  • 20-year monitoring of 6 biological groups over 100 km2 at Ducke
  • decrease in abundance (9 – 35 %) and richness (8 – 25 %) along time, with some taxonomic and functional reorganization
  • more extensive changes in the valleys for trees and ants, and in first-order streams for fish
  • bird and small trees had different patterns of composition change in valleys and plateaus
  • forest and streams are becoming more dynamic as the hydrological cycle intensifies due to climate changes
  • suggest riparian areas in the valleys are hydrological refuges during droughts

Castilho et al. (2006)

Abstract:

  • 72 1-ha plots tree and palm AGB ~ soil gradients (texture & nutrients) and topography (altitude & slope)
  • 8% to 98% sand, slopes 0.5° to 27°, altitude range 70m
  • AGB (palm+trees>1cm) 327.8 Mg/ha (210.9-426.3)
  • two-fold variation among plots, soil or topography explaining 20%
  • AGB ~ +%clay +altitude !=slope
  • slope explained 14% of the AGB in understory & emergent

Castilho et al. (2010)

Abstract:

  • 72 1-ha plots over 2-yr: soil & topographic ~ AGB change
  • AGB increased over 2-yr 1.65 Mg/ha/yr (1.15-2.79)
  • understory & sub-canopy higher rates
  • rate != soil or topography but ~ year of measurement

Esteban et al. (2020)

Abstract:

  • Demography 30 abundant tree species >20years, impacts of droughts & extreme wet periods, and topograpy (shallow to deep WT) with functional traits on climate effects
  • Dry & wet extremes ~ -growth +mortality. But higher impact of interaction.
  • Despite more drought-tolerant, species deep WT were more negatively affected than hydraulically vulnerable species shallow WT.
  • Interaction of wet–dry extremes and WT depth modulated tree responses to climate, providing buffers to droughts in Amazonia.

Garcia et al. (2021)

Abstract:

  • ITV LMA, WD, xylem embolism resistance 2 dominant species along water and light availability accounting tree age and height.
  • ITV hydraulic ~ community variation
  • ITV hydraulic ~ hydrological env, +embolism resistance in deep than shallow WT
  • ITV LMA & WD ~ age, height, light
  • uncoupled coordination among carbon acquisition/conservation and water-use traits

Toledo et al. (2016)

Abstract:

  • soil, species composition, and trait distribution among 72 1-ha plots with fixed climate and control soil with plots design
  • Soil => composition shift => CWM WD
  • (40% AGB, 24% mortality, 18% coarse wood production) ~ clay, CEC, CWM WD, NMDS1
  • Lower biomass and higher mortality on sandy soils is a consequence of dominance of species with low to medium wood density adapted to waterlogged and nutrient-poor sandy soils.
  • Mesoscale variation in biomass and dynamics is caused by co-occurrence of species with similar traits in different parts of the edaphic gradient
